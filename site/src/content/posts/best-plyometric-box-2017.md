Some of the best and most effective plyometric training techniques, however, incorporate not only your bodyweight, but gravity as well. And the best way to bring gravity into the fold is to use a plyometric box (or plyo box for short).</p>

<p><a href="https://fitmus-sport.com/product/wooden-trapezium-plyo-boxes-12-18-24-30/" target="_blank">Best Traditional Plyo Boxes</a></p>
<p>Unlike the multi-sided boxes above, <a href="https://fitmus-sport.com/product/wooden-trapezium-plyo-boxes-12-18-24-30/" target="_blank">traditional plyo boxes</a> have a single usable surface. This means that one box provides one height to work with. Also, this type of plyo box is often trapezoid in shape (wider base, narrower top) with the reason being that it’s a safer design. When you’re performing box jumps, if you ‘miss’ on a jump (fail to jump high enough) you can catch your shins on the lip of the jumping surface, which usually then causes you to fall forward and grate your shins down the edge of the box. This is how many plyometrics enthusiasts end up with badly bloodied shins. The trapezoid shape makes this kind of accident less likely.</p>
